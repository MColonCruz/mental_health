Puerto Rico's national suicide prevention hotline received at least 533,060 calls from July 2014 to June 2020.

About 1 out of 100 calls made to Línea PAS is a silent call. The person reaching for help does not say a word.

Assistance cannot be provided. Information cannot be collected. A crisis cannot be prevented and understood. A story is untold.

A personal exploration of silence and mental health

Knotting what numbers cannot describe

By María de los Milagros Colón Cruz

Let's call her Laura, 29. She was lying on her bed, rocking from side to side, drowning in tears. Laura thought for months that she could handle depression on her own... until she couldn't. She grabbed her phone while somehow feeling in her stomach that she shouldn't be there, that she had no choice but to ask for psychiatric hospitalization if possible. Laura was so scared of those violent images looping through her thoughts. She dialed 1-800-981-0023 –the phone number of the national suicide prevention hotline– let it ring for a while and hung up. She didn't even wait for a "hello".

Diane, 44, hung up the phone because "I felt I could not talk at that moment." "Admitting that we are vulnerable, that we are human and that we have problems is not easy, especially in a culture where we are constantly being pushed to be strong," she says.

Silent Calls Made to Línea PAS Increased After the Pandemic

While silent calls increased by 120%, total calls did by 271%. The data is presented in fiscal years, which start on July.

In a course of five years, from July 2014 to June 2020, Línea PAS received at leasts 528,328 calls – there is no data available for the period between July 2017 and June 2018. From those calls, 4,732 were silent calls. It seems insignificant as those are only .9% of the total rings. But the silence then becomes a big question mark as no details are available about them: how long did they last? How are they treated? Can the organization identify where those calls come from? What happens right after the silence?

When silence is not the absence of noise

I, too, couldn't talk. Translating feelings, signs, and symptoms into words is not only challenging but also a process filled with barriers: fear, shame, stigma, and lack of access to adequate services, among others. "Many times, I feel that I annoy people talking about it," says Raúl, 29, who has also used the Línea PAS.

I, too, looked calm and still. "As women, we have to put up with it and put up with it and put up with it even if they tell us that we are the emotional ones. We deal with the discourse that we are hysterical, but also that we have to put up with everything, and something has to break along the way," argues Diane.

Silence is not necessarily the absence of noise. I, too, had to find a way to ease the internal racket, and there was when my relationship with macramé started. Macramé is a crafting technique that uses knots rather than weaving or knitting to produce textile pieces. And I'm choosing this technique to chart silence, highlight it, and put it in the center.

Five Years of Calls to Línea PA

Reading the piece:

Each square knot represents approximately 400 calls.

There are 29 lines with 46 jute knots, making a little over 533,000.

As the total calls were 528,328, some knots are hidden to avoid overrepresentation. However, keeping them safeguards the functionality of the piece.

Twelve knots at the center represent the silent calls.

But it is fine. Counting knots is not as relevant as centering this converstion on those who called and stayed silent. This piece is just a bridge.

Counting and narrating mental health struggles

The previous visualization shows official data. That data was collected and published by the agency in charge of the Línea PAS – Oficina de Calidad de la Administración de Servicios de Salud y Contra la Adicción (ASSMCA)– but it does not necessarily tell a story or make you feel part of it. This data does not reach the nuances of mental health.

Intending to fill this gap, I decided to collect personal data, small and absolutely imperfect data about my daily struggles. Showing it here is also an effort to crack the barriers

Episodes in 48 Hours: A Prototype

I collected hourly data for three categories: depression signs and symptoms, anxiety signs and symptoms, and coping strategies. Every element in each category was classified as true or false. Each horizontal line represents one hour, and the maximum amount of trues for an hour determines the width of the piece. In addition, a different color will represent each category. The image on the right is my point of reference, whereas the one on the left is a failed attempt.

Mixed feelings about Línea PAS

Laura expected more from her call. She thought she would receive some kind of assessment and a referral, but that didn't happen. Still, she appreciated the space to blow off some steam. Raúl thinks withouth a doubt that Línea PAS is one of the very few public programs that work in Puerto Rico. And Diane says that mostly competent and sensible people tended to her and also highlithed how helpful the venting sessions can be.

"Sometimes just speaking and having someone listen to you and not judging, well that's enough, especially for single people, like me, who don't have a lot of friends and family members. I don't have people who are really close to me, and I live alone. So, there are times when your cup overflows and you don't know who to talk to. And I've found that the Línea PAS has been a great alternative," says Diane.

But not all stories are the same. For Karla, 33, Línea PAS “is not a line that offers any real help." In her experience, she had to insist on being referred to specific services, and she thinks it's a matter of luck whether or not one lands with a good counselor.

In any case, Karla concludes that she would recommend using the hotline to someone in crisis. "I would not tell them not to call, but I recognize that they would need more than that."
